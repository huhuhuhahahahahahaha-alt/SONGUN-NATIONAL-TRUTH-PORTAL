NEVER DELIVERED
My dearest daughter, whose name I say to myself each morning so that I do not forget that I am a person —

Within these walls I have learned the texture of silence. Not the silence of evening or of a sleeping child — this kind is different. It is the silence of ten thousand people who have learned that sound costs something. We do not speak during morning assembly. We do not speak during labor. We have forgotten the weight of ordinary conversation. I keep yours alive inside me: the way you said omma when you were angry, the way it became three syllables instead of two.

I want you to know that I chose wrongly. Not in the thing they said I did — I still do not fully understand what that was. But in believing that loyalty could protect us. I raised you to bow deeply at assemblies, to clap with full arms, to mean it when you sang. I thought this was armor. It was not armor. It was a tax. And when the tax became insufficient, it was never armor at all. It was only obedience they collected before taking what they wanted regardless.

The guard who brings water each morning is perhaps twenty years old. He does not look at us. I have watched him for eight months and he has never once met a prisoner's eyes. I think they train this. The ability to not see. But his hands shake very slightly when he pours — just enough that I know somewhere inside him the mechanism is still intact. He knows he is pouring water for human beings. He has only learned to perform as though he does not.

Nothing prepared me for the first winter. Cold here is not cold. It is a presence with patience and intention and all the time it needs. Three people in my block did not survive it. Their absence was not acknowledged by anyone with authority to acknowledge it. One morning they existed. The next morning their spaces were empty and no one said their names. I said their names. I am still saying them. I will tell you their names if I ever see your face again.

Every morning I perform a mathematics I cannot fully explain — I calculate from the season and my best guess of the year how old you are now, and I try to build you in my mind at that age, with that height, with that face. Memory wears down like stone beneath water. The edges first, then the center. I do not know if you are safe. I do not know if you were taken to a different facility, which is the law for the families of those classified as I am. I do not know if you are alive. I say your name each morning so I do not forget that you exist. That I existed for someone.

Since they brought me here I have not seen the sky. The work yard has a roof. The dormitory has no windows. I know from the quality of the grey light through the panels when it is winter and when it is not, but I have not seen a cloud, a bird, the open sky. I think about it constantly. The specific blue of three in the afternoon in early October — which I have decided is the finest thing in the world, finer than anything I previously thought was fine. When I am released — if I am — I will lie down on the ground outside and look up until I have finished.

Somehow I still believe you will read this. Not rationally — I know the probability is very small. But something in the mind refuses to write to no one. Something insists on the fiction of delivery. So I maintain it. You will read this. You will know I was here. That I thought of you every hour. That I was not broken — bent considerably, yes — but not broken. And that I loved you with a fullness that no reclassification, no document, no loyalty score, no decree could reach, because it existed in a part of me that they have no form for and no jurisdiction over. They can take the sky. They cannot take what is already given.

With everything I have remaining —
Your mother
p.s. — In the block, the guards number us. They do not use our names. After long enough, you begin to number yourself. I resist this each morning by speaking your name aloud before the assembly bell. Some mornings I also speak the names of the others in my block. Seven of us remain. I say all seven. It takes eleven seconds. I have measured it. Eleven seconds of resistance is what I am allowed. It has to be enough.

She showed me her notebook. Every entry was food. What she ate. What her children ate. Nothing for three days, then half a corn husk shared between four people. Her youngest had the distended belly — the kind that happens when the body begins consuming itself. She called it "swamp disease" because she had never been taught the word malnutrition. The model village that foreign observers visited had been specially stocked for weeks in advance. The same village. Every inspection. Every delegation. Every time. The people who lived there had rehearsed their happiness. They had been told which facial expressions to wear, which words to use, what to say if asked whether they had enough. They were performing abundance for an audience that needed to believe it, and everyone in the room understood this, and no one said so.

FINAL TRANSMISSION — DEFECTOR FILE 009
RECORDED: [CLASSIFIED LOCATION]  ·  SPEAKER: IDENTITY PERMANENTLY PROTECTED  ·  TRANSLATED FROM KOREAN  ·  VERIFIED

I was eleven years old when I became a Youth Loyalty Monitor. They gave us a badge. A red badge with a small gold star at its center. We were supposed to report classmates who spoke improperly about the Leader — who showed insufficient enthusiasm at assemblies, who failed to cry convincingly at state funerals, who mentioned that their rations had been smaller than last season's.

I reported a girl in my class. She had said, quietly, to a friend, after school, that the corn rations were smaller than last year. She had observed a fact. Her family disappeared three weeks later. I received a certificate of merit signed by our district coordinator. I kept that certificate for twelve years. I was proud of it. Then I crossed the river and I began, slowly, to understand what I had been trained into.

People ask me when I first doubted. I tell them: I never doubted, in the way that implies I had the option not to. Doubt requires an architecture of thinking that I had never been given the materials to build. The possibility of questioning — the idea that an authorized truth might be a limited truth — was not suppressed in me. It was simply never installed. You cannot remove a tool that was never placed in the house.

When I finally crossed, I had to relearn how to think. Not what to think. How. The practice of forming an opinion without first checking whether it is permitted. The machinery of doubt. The muscle of skepticism. I was thirty-seven years old, in a cold apartment in a country that did not want me either, learning to have a private thought for the first time in my life.

People ask me what I miss. I tell them the truth, which is the hardest answer: I miss the certainty. That is what the state gave us. The removal of ambiguity. The warm, complete, total knowledge of exactly who you are, exactly what you must do, exactly why your life has meaning — because the banner tells you. Because the poster shows you. When you lose the banner you lose the answer. And you discover, for the first time, that you never learned how to ask the question.

When I finally crossed the Tumen River, the water was cold enough to stop my hands from working properly. I had nothing. No document, no money, no language. On the other side was China — which I had been taught my entire life was an enemy state. I stood on the bank and understood that everything I had been told about this place, about the people, about what lay beyond our borders — I had never verified a single piece of it. I had never been permitted to.
FRAGMENT — [SEQUENCE UNKNOWN]
My father died in our apartment in the winter of what I now calculate was 1996 or 1997 — the years that later became known abroad as the famine. He was forty-one. The official cause of death recorded by the district health officer was "natural causes." I did not know the word malnutrition until I left the country. The state did not supply us with the vocabulary necessary to describe what it was doing to us. Without the word, we could not name it. Without a name, we could not think it. Without a thought, we could not object.
FRAGMENT — [SEQUENCE UNKNOWN]
It took eleven years to reach South Korea. I moved through China, Laos, Thailand, and three detention facilities. I was sold twice by brokers who had helped me cross — people I had trusted because I had no other option, and who understood this about me and priced it accordingly. I arrived in Seoul at thirty-one. At a resettlement center, a social worker told me I could choose my own apartment. I asked her who I needed to ask permission from. She said: no one. This was the first thing she said that I truly could not understand.
FRAGMENT — [SEQUENCE UNKNOWN]
I was born in Hyesan, a border city in the far north of the country. My family had a Songbun classification of "wavering" — my grandfather had relatives in South Korea who he had never met, from before the war, from before the border existed. This was my inheritance: not land, not money, not name — but suspicion. Permanent, documented, hereditary suspicion. I was four years old before I understood that not all children were watched the way we were watched.
FRAGMENT — [SEQUENCE UNKNOWN]
In China I watched a television broadcast from South Korea on a screen in a restaurant. I did not understand most of it. But there were ordinary people, walking ordinary streets, and none of them looked afraid. I had been taught that South Korea was a place of misery and oppression, that its citizens groaned under foreign military subjugation and begged to be reunited with the Motherland. The people on the screen did not look like they were begging for anything. This was the first fact I encountered that I knew, with absolute certainty, was the direct opposite of what I had been taught.
FRAGMENT — [SEQUENCE UNKNOWN]
The markets — the jangmadang — began to grow in the mid-nineties when the public distribution system collapsed and the state simply could not feed us. For a period, trading anything was technically illegal. You could be jailed for selling food. People sold food anyway because the alternative was to die, and most people, when given the choice between an illegal act and death, will choose the illegal act. What the state could not understand — or would not acknowledge — was that it had created a generation that had learned, quietly, in the markets, that survival sometimes required disobedience.

NOVEMBER · TUMEN RIVER · AIR TEMPERATURE: −14°C
Hyeonseo Lee · Crossed 1997 · Age 17

I had lived my entire life three hundred meters from the river. I knew its shape in every season. I had never thought of it as a border — it was simply the edge of the world. Then one night I waded in and walked across, and the world became larger. It took eleven years to reach a country that would not send me back. I arrived in Seoul at twenty-eight and called my mother from a phone booth. She did not know I was alive. She had spent eleven years not knowing if I was alive.

Park Yeon-mi · Crossed 2007 · Age 13

My father had smuggled himself across first to arrange passage. My mother and I followed at night. I weighed thirty-two kilograms. A broker paid the border guard to look away. We crossed in darkness and reached a safe house in a Chinese village. We had been told that in China we would be safe. It is not unusual. It is a system.

TESTIMONY · 04 OF 06
SPRING · AFTER 10 YEARS OF PLANNING · ALONE
Kang Chol-hwan · Crossed 1992 · Age 22

I had spent ten years in Camp 15 — from age nine to nineteen — because my grandfather had expressed ideological doubt. When I was eventually released I spent three years planning the crossing. On the night I crossed I swam rather than waded, because the water was too shallow to avoid border guards from a walking position. I made it across. On the Chinese side I lived hidden for two years, moving every few weeks. I could not announce myself. I did not legally exist. The People's Republic of China did not recognize North Korean defectors as refugees. They still do not. They return them. They have always returned them.

TESTIMONY · 05 OF 06
WINTER · SIX MONTHS PREGNANT · FIVE OTHERS WITH HER
Kim Ji-eun · Composite · Drawn from multiple accounts

She crossed in her sixth month because staying had become impossible — her husband had been taken, and pregnant women without husbands were subject to surveillance that would not end well. She waded through chest-height water in February. When she reached the Chinese bank she could not stand without assistance. The child was born six weeks later in a village that hid her in a barn. The child was not registered. An unregistered child born to a North Korean mother in China has no legal status anywhere on Earth. The child is now fourteen years old. She crossed last year, alone, using a route her mother had mapped for her on a piece of cloth.

TESTIMONY · 06 OF 06
AFTER REACHING SAFETY · WENT BACK · ONCE
Lee Hyun-seo · Composite · Multiple documented cases

She reached Thailand after eighteen months in China and was processed for resettlement in South Korea. In Seoul she learned that her younger brother and mother were still in the northern provinces — her mother had not been able to cross when she did, and her brother was too young to cross alone. She spent four years working and saving, then hired a broker to bring them out. The broker was captured at the river with both of them. She spent her entire savings bribing officials to secure their release. It took three years. It nearly did not work. When they finally arrived in Seoul her mother did not recognize her at first. She had been gone so long that her face had changed. Her mother touched it with both hands to make sure.

DOCUMENTED · VERIFIED · REAL

Between 1990 and the present, an estimated 300,000 North Koreans have crossed into China. The majority have been caught and repatriated. Those returned face interrogation, detention, and in many cases execution — treated under North Korean law as traitors for the act of leaving.

The river is ninety meters wide at its narrowest crossing point. Some nights, border guards look away. Most nights, they do not.

The names in the memorial that follows are the names of those who did not make it back. They are not all the names. They are the names we were given.
